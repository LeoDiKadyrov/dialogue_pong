# Dialogue Pong — Positioning Statement
*Final draft as of 2026-03-26. Based on 6 respondents: creator (x2), wife, 4 friends.*

---

## The customer test
The person who picks Dialogue Pong wants to connect with a stranger but hates starting conversations. They don't need another chat box — they need something to do while their guard drops. The Pong mechanic solves the opener problem: you're already playing, already committed, already typing something. The conversation starts before you decide to have one.

Secondary frame: people fed up with random video chat — the webcams, the microphones, the jerkers-off. Text-only with a mandatory reply mechanic removes all of that by design.

## The honest gap
Three barriers:
1. **Cold-start** — matchmaking only works with enough simultaneous players. Solved through community-first launch, not product changes.
2. **"Is this a scam?"** — anonymous stranger site with an unknown URL triggers distrust. Familiar Pong visuals and immediate game load help. Kill this in the first 5 seconds of the landing page.
3. **"Why Pong if I just want to talk?"** — the game mechanic feels like friction until you understand it's the icebreaker. Lead with the problem (hard to start conversations), not the mechanic.

## The core bet
Build community around the idea. The product is done. The next phase is getting enough people online simultaneously — scheduled sessions, seeded communities, distributing to people curious enough to click something unusual. The audience isn't searching for a chat app. They're open to something weird.

## Market scope
Post-Omegle stranger chat, with overlap into social connection tools for introverts. Market vote across 6 respondents: A (chat) = 3, B (experimental games) = 1, C (social connection) = 2. Positioning serves both A and C: the same mechanic that removes jerkers-off also solves "I don't know what to say."

## Positioning statement

> We help people who want to connect with a stranger but hate starting conversations, through a retro game that breaks the ice before you realize it's happening.

*(30 words)*

---

## Key insight: the icebreaker engine
Three respondents independently named this without being asked: the game solves "I don't know how to start." You don't arrive needing something to say. Pong gives you something to do while your guard drops. Spontaneity happens because there's no pressure to perform — you just have to reply before the timer runs out. This is the mechanic's deepest value, and it's underrepresented in the current statement.

## Product feedback (not positioning — but real)
From respondents, separate from positioning:
- Gameplay lacks clarity: no obvious win/lose state, unclear if ball speeds up (Friend 3)
- 30-second timer feels too short given ball travel time (Friend 4)

---

## Distribution idea worth exploring
**Twitch streamers + subscribers** (Friend 3): streamer plays 1v1 with a subscriber, game as the frame for the conversation. Low stimulation for live stream, but could work as recorded content. Reference: Dota YouTuber "Vasya Ne Dendy" played paid 1v1s with subscribers and published the conversations.

---

## Competitive context
- Closest competitors: Emerald Chat, Chatroulette, Monkey
- White space: text-only (no webcam/mic), enforced turn-based pacing, mandatory reply, goal is NOT winning, no skipping
- Omegle shut down Nov 2023 — every replacement is a video clone. No text-first redesign exists.
- Launch channels: itch.io, indie game communities, "weird internet things" spaces, potentially Twitch content creators

---

## What not to lead with
- Don't open with "talk to strangers" — invites "why Pong then?"
- Don't open with "retro game" — loses the connection-seeker before they understand
- Lead with the problem: hard to start conversations, or random video chat is exhausting and gross
- Then: this is the thing that starts the conversation for you
